Eighteen Grove — Approach

How we partner,
step by step.

Our approach is a way of partnering, not a transaction template. It runs from the first conversation through every year we own a business alongside the people who run it.

What actually happens

Four steps, no
surprises.

Selling or partnering on a business you built is daunting. Here is exactly how it goes with us — and where you stay in control the whole way.

Step one

A confidential first call

We talk, privately. We'll sign an NDA on request before you share anything sensitive, and nothing leaves the room without your say-so. No bankers required to say hello.

Step two

An indication of value — no strings

Within a few weeks we give you an honest read on value. No obligation, and no exclusivity to start. If it's not a fit, you've lost nothing.

Step three

You choose the path

Full sale, partial sale, or a value-added partnership where you keep building. Your team hears about any change from you, not from us.

Step four

Management stays; you stay as involved as you want

Your people keep their jobs and their leaders. You deal directly with the founder throughout — and we don't retrade absent a material, undisclosed problem.

The first 100 days

What changes,
and what doesn't.

How we grow a business with its team, not over it. These are methods and intentions — the levers we bring, not outcomes we promise.

✓

What we add

Clearer KPI reporting, pricing discipline, selective key hires (a CFO or GM where one is needed), better systems, and add-on acquisitions to grow the platform.

—

What we don't touch

Your people, your name, your culture, and the relationships that made the business work. We change what's broken, not what's working.

Ownership TransitionsGuideJune 2026

Recap, partial sale, or full exit: a founder's guide to ownership transitions

Most founder- and family-owned businesses reach a point where the owner starts to think about what comes next. The good news: "selling" isn't a single decision — it's a spectrum, and the right point on it depends on what you want for yourself, your family, and your team.

The three paths

A full sale. You sell the business outright and step away on a timeline you choose. It's the cleanest exit, and the right one when you're ready to fully retire or move on. A good buyer keeps your management, protects the name, and gives your people continuity.

A partial sale or recapitalization. You sell a portion of the business and roll the rest into the next chapter — taking meaningful chips off the table while keeping equity, and upside, in what comes next. This suits owners who want liquidity but aren't done building.

A value-added partnership. You stay at the helm and bring on a partner for capital, experience, and support. The goal is growth, not exit; you remain the driver.

How to choose

The right path usually comes down to three questions: How much do you want to step back — now, versus over time? How important is keeping equity in the future of the business? And who do you trust to be the next steward of what you've built? Price matters, but for most owners we meet, it's rarely the first thing, or the only thing.

A note on timing

There's no wrong time to have a confidential, no-obligation conversation. Understanding your options early — even years before you act — almost always leads to a better outcome than waiting until you're forced to move.

Eighteen Grove partners with founder- and family-owned businesses across services and distribution. If you're weighing what comes next, we're glad to talk it through — privately, and with no expectation.
